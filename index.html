Suicides Among Higher Education Students

Estimating suicide among higher education students in England and Wales

Background

Suicide, the second-leading cause of death among college students, claims the lives of approximately 5000 college-age people each year. The number is within the minimum estimate because of many suicide cases reported as accidents.

Over the years, researchers have analyzed a variety of issues concerning college student suicide, including causes, rate and solutions. The Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC, 1995) claims that suicide is the number-two killer of people between 20-24.[1]

A sample of 1455 college students from four higher education institutions was collected to examine issues of depression and suicide.[2]

According to the American College Health Association, 1 in 10 college students reported having seriously considered suicide in the previous year.[3]

Suicide Definition

The National Statistics definition of suicide includes all deaths from intentional self-harm for persons aged 10 years and over, and deaths where the intent was undetermined for those aged 15 years and over.

Research Problem

According to the Office for National Statistics (ONS), students in higher Education in England and Wales kill themselves at the rate of almost two every week. All of the above indicates that suicide among higher education students happens relatively frequently. Recognizing the seriousness of the problem, the dataset containing estimates of suicides among higher education students of England and Wales by sex, age and ethnicity from July 2001 to July 2017 is chosen to be analyzed and visualized, aiming to show the statistical results and provide insight into the issue of higher education student suicides

Conclusion

Here are some overall points:

  1. The rate of suicide in the 12 months ending July 2017 for higher education students in England and Wales was 4.7 deaths per 100,000 students, which equates to 95 suicides; this is higher than in most of the earlier years studied, although the small numbers per year make it difficult to identify statistically significant differences.
  2. Between the 12 months ending July 2013 and the 12 months ending July 2016, higher education students in England and Wales had a significantly lower suicide rate compared with the general population of similar ages.
  3. Male higher education students had a significantly higher rate of suicide compared with female students.
  4. Suicide rate of higher education students tends to be greater in senior age group.

Sarah Caul, Senior Research Officer for the ONS, said:

"To help provide insight into the issue of student suicides we have produced new analysis examining suicide trends among higher education students.

"The rate of suicide in 2016 to 2017 in higher education students was 4.7 deaths per 100,000 students. Although higher than in earlier years, the comparatively low numbers of suicides per year make it challenging to identify significant differences.
